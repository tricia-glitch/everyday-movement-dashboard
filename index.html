Before You Read
Essential context to get the most from this novel

★ What This Book Is

Everyday Movement is an award-winning novel set during the 2019 Hong Kong anti-extradition protests. It follows two college roommates—the quiet, anxious Ah Lei and the brash, self-assured Panda—as their friendship, families, and daily lives are reshaped by the political upheaval around them.

The novel interweaves scenes of normalcy (meals, shopping, movies) with episodes of tear gas, violence, and moral reckoning. Originally published in Chinese, it won Taiwan's Golden Tripod Award—the country's highest literary honor—and was notably removed from the Hong Kong Book Fair in 2024 after political complaints.

Content Heads-Up

This novel includes depictions of protest violence, tear gas exposure, police confrontation, political tension within families, and emotional distress. Leung portrays these events with realism and visceral sensory detail.

■ Hong Kong: A Brief FAQ

You don't need to be an expert to read this novel, but a few basics will deepen your understanding of what's at stake.

Why was Hong Kong different from the rest of China?
When Britain returned Hong Kong to China in 1997, the agreement guaranteed freedoms of press, speech, and assembly until 2047 under a framework called "one country, two systems." The 2019 protests were about the fear that this promise was being broken decades early.
What triggered the 2019 protests?
A proposed extradition bill that would have allowed Hong Kong residents to be sent to mainland China for trial. Over a million people marched against it. The government suspended the bill, but by then the movement had expanded into broader demands for democracy and accountability.
How big were the protests?
Massive. In a city of 7.5 million, an estimated 2 million marched on a single day. The movement lasted months, with tear gas, mass arrests, and clashes becoming part of daily life—which is exactly what the novel captures.
What happened after?
In 2020, Beijing imposed a sweeping National Security Law criminalizing dissent. Dozens of activists were arrested, organizations dissolved, and a wave of emigration began. This novel was removed from the Hong Kong Book Fair in 2024 under political pressure. Leung now lives in Taiwan.
Do I need to know all this to enjoy the book?
No. Leung wrote the novel so you experience the protests the way her characters do—from inside daily life, without a textbook framing. The confusion, the sensory overload, the not-knowing-what-to-do: that's the point. This FAQ is just here if you want grounding.

♦ Key Concepts to Know

Leung doesn't over-explain Hong Kong for outsiders. These terms and ideas will enrich your reading:

"One Country, Two Systems"
The framework promised when Britain returned Hong Kong to China in 1997—guaranteeing freedoms of press, speech, and assembly until 2047.
"Be Water"
Borrowed from Bruce Lee, this became the protest motto: stay fluid, avoid direct confrontation, disperse and regroup. No single leader, no fixed location.
Lennon Walls
Public walls covered with sticky notes carrying messages of solidarity. Named after the original in Prague, they appeared across Hong Kong neighborhoods.
Yellow vs. Blue Economy
Businesses color-coded by political allegiance. "Yellow" shops supported protesters; "blue" shops were pro-government. Where you ate lunch became a political act.
New Town Plaza
The shopping mall in Sha Tin where the novel opens. In real life, it was a major protest flashpoint—a symbol of how consumerism and resistance occupied the same space.
"Five Demands, Not One Less"
The protest slogan: withdraw the bill, retract the "riot" label, release arrested protesters, investigate police, and implement universal suffrage.

♣ How to Read This Novel

This isn't a protest novel with heroes and villains. Leung uses a kaleidoscopic structure—shifting between characters, moments, and moods without guiding you toward a single conclusion.

Let the fragments accumulate. The power is in the juxtaposition: a mother washing her daughter's tear-gas-stained clothes, then going to buy groceries. A couple arguing about whether to attend a march, then watching a movie. The "everyday" IS the movement. That's the entire point of the title.

Reviews & Essays
Critical perspectives on the novel — read before or after the book

“The Human Texture of Protest”

Jennifer Eagleton · Cha: An Asian Literary Journal · Jan 2026

A deep-dive review exploring how Leung renders the 2019 protests not through grand political narrative but through the sensory, emotional grain of ordinary life—tear gas mixing with the smell of cooking, the weight of a phone full of protest footage. Eagleton examines how this novel makes the political deeply personal.

“A Love Letter to Hong Kong”

Susan Blumberg-Kason · Cha: An Asian Literary Journal · Dec 2025

A first-impressions essay that situates Everyday Movement as an act of literary devotion to the city itself—not just to the protests, but to the neighborhoods, rhythms, and relationships that define Hong Kong. Blumberg-Kason considers what it means to write about a place you can no longer safely return to.

“The Latest Chapter: Post-2019 Fiction from Hong Kong”

Words Without Borders · March 2026

A wider literary essay that places Everyday Movement alongside other post-2019 Hong Kong fiction, examining how a generation of writers is processing political upheaval through literature—and what it means when that literature is banned at home but published abroad.
About the Author
Gigi L. Leung (Leung Lee Chi / 梁莉姿)

✎ Who She Is

Gigi L. Leung grew up in Hong Kong, graduated from the Chinese University of Hong Kong, and now lives in Hualien, Taiwan. She began writing at fifteen about individuals and the times in which they live. She is among the youngest award-winning authors in Hong Kong's literary scene and writes both fiction and poetry.

Golden Tripod Award 2024 (Taiwan) Young Artist Award 2020 (HK Arts Council) Taipei Literary Award Finalist

★ Why This Book Matters

Everyday Movement was ordered removed from the Hong Kong Book Fair in 2024 after political complaints—an act that underscores its power and relevance. For English-language readers, this is one of the first major works of fiction about the 2019 protests to reach a wide international audience.

Translator Jennifer Feeley, known for her translations of Hong Kong writer Xi Xi, brings a deep understanding of Hong Kong's literary voice to the English text.

These aren't background music—they're companion pieces. Each song maps onto something specific in the novel: a scene, a tension, a feeling Leung builds across the book. Play them while you read, or after.

願榮光歸香港
Anonymous / 2019 Protest Anthem
Protest
Connection to the book: This is the anthem that emerged from the movement the novel depicts. Composed anonymously and crowd-performed in shopping malls—the same malls where Leung's characters shop, eat, and try to maintain normalcy. When you hear it, you're hearing the sound the characters hear. The song was later criminalized under Hong Kong's national security law.
2
Boundless Oceans, Vast Skies
海闊天空
Beyond
Identity
Connection to the book: Hong Kong's unofficial anthem of perseverance. Beyond wrote it in 1993 about chasing dreams against impossible odds; by 2019 it had become the song protesters sang in the streets. It captures the central tension of the novel: the desire for freedom colliding with the reality of what it costs. Ah Lei's uncertainty and Panda's defiance are both inside this song.
3
Wedding Card Street
喜帖街
Kay Tse (謝安琪)
Nostalgia
Connection to the book: Kay Tse mourns a specific Hong Kong street being demolished for redevelopment—exactly the kind of loss Leung writes about. The novel is saturated with named places: MTR stations, shopping malls, university campuses. This song captures what it feels like when the physical city you know starts disappearing, which is the backdrop to everything the characters experience.
4
Rolls-Royce
勞斯·萊斯
Denise Ho (何韵詩)
Protest
Connection to the book: Denise Ho is the real-life version of the choices Leung's characters face. A Cantopop star who was blacklisted from mainland China after supporting the Umbrella Movement, she lost her commercial career but became a symbol of artistic conscience. The novel asks what you're willing to sacrifice; Ho's life answers it. She was later arrested under the national security law.
5
Every Time You Leave

My Little Airport
Loss
Connection to the book: My Little Airport is the sound of the generation Leung writes about—young, melancholic, fiercely local. This lo-fi track captures the quiet ache of departures that runs through the novel: friends leaving Hong Kong, relationships fracturing under political pressure, the slow realization that the city you grew up in might not be there to come back to. It's the emotional frequency of Ah Lei's interior world.

Film Pairings
Three films that deepen the world of this novel

Revolution of Our Times (2021)

Directed by Kiwi Chow · Documentary · 2h 32m

This documentary was filmed secretly during the 2019 protests and smuggled out of Hong Kong. It shows the actual events Leung fictionalizes—the tear gas, the mall confrontations, the exhausted young people trying to figure out what to do next. Watch it after the novel and you'll recognize every scene. It won Best Documentary at the Golden Horse Awards and is banned in Hong Kong.

WHY IT PAIRS: Puts real footage to the moments Leung renders in prose. You'll understand why she chose fiction—it lets her go where the camera can't, inside her characters' heads.

In the Mood for Love (2000)

Directed by Wong Kar-wai · Drama · 1h 38m

Wong Kar-wai's masterpiece about two neighbors in 1960s Hong Kong who discover their spouses are having an affair. On its surface, it's a love story. But underneath, it's about how a city shapes the way people hold back, perform normalcy, and ache privately. That's exactly what Leung's characters do under political pressure instead of romantic pressure.

WHY IT PAIRS: The emotional grammar is the same—restraint, longing, and the weight of things left unsaid. Leung writes Hong Kong the way Wong Kar-wai films it: through food, corridors, and glances.

Ten Years (2015)

Anthology · Drama/Sci-Fi · 1h 44m

Five short films imagining Hong Kong in 2025—a city where Cantonese is dying, self-immolation is political protest, and taxi drivers are surveilled by the state. Made in 2015, it was dismissed as alarmist. By 2020, much of it had come true. It won Best Film at the Hong Kong Film Awards and was immediately pulled from mainland Chinese cinemas.

WHY IT PAIRS: Watch it before the novel. It shows the fears that were already in the air before 2019—the same fears that drive Leung's characters to march, freeze, or look away.
Food & Drink
Set the mood for reading — the flavors of this novel's world

Food runs through Everyday Movement like a second narrative—meals shared, meals skipped, the politics of where you eat. These are the tastes that accompany the story.

Hong Kong Milk Tea (港式奶茶)

The essential reading companion

Strong black tea filtered through a cloth "stocking," blended with evaporated milk. It's thick, bittersweet, and deeply comforting—the drink cha chaan tengs serve all day. In the novel, ordinary routines like ordering milk tea become acts of continuity. Brew it strong, add condensed or evaporated milk, and drink it while you read.

Egg Waffle (雞蛋仔)

Street food · The taste of walking through Hong Kong

Puffy, golden, bubble-shaped waffles sold at street stalls across the city. They're the flavor of movement itself—something you eat while walking, while deciding where to go, while the city happens around you. In a novel about everyday motion through a city in crisis, this is the snack that fits.

Pineapple Bun (艐蘿包)

Cha chaan teng staple · Comfort in a familiar place

A sweet, crumbly-topped bun with no actual pineapple—named for the pattern on its crust. Served at the cha chaan tengs where Leung's characters sit, talk, and avoid talking. The yellow economy meant that even choosing which bakery to buy one from was political. Slice it open, add a slab of butter, and eat it with your milk tea.

Chrysanthemum Tea (菊花茶)

Cooling · For the heavier chapters

A light, floral, slightly sweet tea traditionally used to "cool" the body. In a novel thick with tear gas and emotional heat, this is the counterbalance. Drink it cold during the protest chapters, hot during the quieter domestic scenes. It's the taste of trying to stay calm when everything around you isn't.
