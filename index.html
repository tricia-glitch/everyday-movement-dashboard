Before You Read
Essential context to get the most from this novel

★ What This Book Is

Everyday Movement is an award-winning novel set during the 2019 Hong Kong anti-extradition protests. It follows two college roommates—the quiet, anxious Ah Lei and the brash, self-assured Panda—as their friendship, families, and daily lives are reshaped by the political upheaval around them.

The novel interweaves scenes of normalcy (meals, shopping, movies) with episodes of tear gas, violence, and moral reckoning. Originally published in Chinese, it won Taiwan's Golden Tripod Award—the country's highest literary honor—and was notably removed from the Hong Kong Book Fair in 2024 after political complaints.

Content Heads-Up

This novel includes depictions of protest violence, tear gas exposure, police confrontation, political tension within families, and emotional distress. Leung portrays these events with realism and visceral sensory detail.

■ The Historical Moment

The entire novel unfolds during the summer and fall of 2019, when Hong Kong experienced the largest protests in its history. Here's the timeline you need:

February 2019

The Extradition Bill is proposed

Hong Kong's government introduces a bill allowing extradition of suspects to mainland China. Critics fear it would expose Hong Kongers to China's opaque legal system and erode the "one country, two systems" framework.

June 9, 2019

One million march

An estimated one million people take to the streets peacefully. The government does not withdraw the bill.

June 12, 2019

First major clashes

Police fire tear gas and rubber bullets at protesters surrounding the Legislative Council. This is the first major escalation—the moment the "everyday" becomes the "movement."

June 15, 2019

Bill suspended, but too late

The government suspends (but doesn't withdraw) the bill. Protesters expand their demands to five, including an independent investigation into police conduct and universal suffrage.

July – November 2019

Escalation & the "be water" strategy

Protests become decentralized. Tear gas becomes routine. Shopping malls become protest sites. This is the world of the novel—where daily life and political crisis blur completely.

June 2020 (after the novel)

National Security Law imposed

Beijing imposes a sweeping security law criminalizing dissent. Many activists are arrested, organizations dissolve, and a wave of emigration begins. This is the shadow that hangs over the English translation.

♦ Key Concepts to Know

Leung doesn't over-explain Hong Kong for outsiders. These terms and ideas will enrich your reading:

"One Country, Two Systems"
The framework promised when Britain returned Hong Kong to China in 1997—guaranteeing freedoms of press, speech, and assembly until 2047.
"Be Water"
Borrowed from Bruce Lee, this became the protest motto: stay fluid, avoid direct confrontation, disperse and regroup. No single leader, no fixed location.
Lennon Walls
Public walls covered with sticky notes carrying messages of solidarity. Named after the original in Prague, they appeared across Hong Kong neighborhoods.
Yellow vs. Blue Economy
Businesses color-coded by political allegiance. "Yellow" shops supported protesters; "blue" shops were pro-government. Where you ate lunch became a political act.
New Town Plaza
The shopping mall in Sha Tin where the novel opens. In real life, it was a major protest flashpoint—a symbol of how consumerism and resistance occupied the same space.
"Five Demands, Not One Less"
The protest slogan: withdraw the bill, retract the "riot" label, release arrested protesters, investigate police, and implement universal suffrage.

♣ How to Read This Novel

This isn't a protest novel with heroes and villains. Leung uses a kaleidoscopic structure—shifting between characters, moments, and moods without guiding you toward a single conclusion.

Let the fragments accumulate. The power is in the juxtaposition: a mother washing her daughter's tear-gas-stained clothes, then going to buy groceries. A couple arguing about whether to attend a march, then watching a movie. The "everyday" IS the movement. That's the entire point of the title.

The Characters
The interconnected lives at the center of this story

Ah Lei

College student · Protagonist

Meek, anxious, deeply affected by the political turmoil. She feels the weight of what's happening and can't look away. Her uncertainty mirrors the broader paralysis many Hong Kongers experienced: wanting to act but unsure how, or at what cost.

Panda

Ah Lei's roommate · Protagonist

Brash, self-assured, and determined to maintain her daily routine despite the chaos. Panda's insistence on normalcy—self-care, shopping, going out—is its own form of resistance, or perhaps denial. Leung lets you decide which.

The Mothers

Family · Supporting

The mother-daughter relationships are central. These women hold families together while the city fractures, navigating the gap between wanting to protect their children and understanding why they march.

The Broader Cast

Interconnected lives

Romantic partners, classmates, shopkeepers, strangers on the MTR. Leung traces how the movement touches everyone differently—financial stress, ethical dilemmas, fractured friendships, quiet acts of courage.

About the Author
Gigi L. Leung (Leung Lee Chi / 梁莉姿)

✎ Who She Is

Gigi L. Leung grew up in Hong Kong, graduated from the Chinese University of Hong Kong, and now lives in Hualien, Taiwan. She began writing at fifteen about individuals and the times in which they live. She is among the youngest award-winning authors in Hong Kong's literary scene and writes both fiction and poetry.

Golden Tripod Award 2024 (Taiwan)Young Artist Award 2020 (HK Arts Council)Taipei Literary Award Finalist

★ Why This Book Matters

Everyday Movement was ordered removed from the Hong Kong Book Fair in 2024 after political complaints—an act that underscores its power and relevance. For English-language readers, this is one of the first major works of fiction about the 2019 protests to reach a wide international audience.

Translator Jennifer Feeley, known for her translations of Hong Kong writer Xi Xi, brings a deep understanding of Hong Kong's literary voice to the English text.

10 Questions for Your Book Group
Tap any question to reveal a discussion prompt underneath
1
Panda keeps shopping, getting her nails done, and going to the movies while the city burns. Is her insistence on normalcy a form of resistance—or a form of cowardice?
Consider how Leung presents Panda without judgment. Is maintaining daily life during crisis an act of self-preservation, privilege, denial, or quiet defiance? Have you ever kept your routine going during a crisis, and what did that mean for you?
Tap for discussion prompt
2
The novel suggests that what you DON'T do during a political crisis matters as much as what you do. Where in your own life have you felt the weight of inaction?
Kirkus noted this as a central theme. Think about moments in your community, workplace, or country where silence or inaction carried moral weight. Does Leung seem to believe neutrality is possible?
Tap for discussion prompt
3
Leung deliberately avoids telling you who is "right." Did that frustrate you—or did it make the novel more powerful? Should political fiction take sides?
Compare this to other political novels you've read. Does presenting multiple perspectives without judgment make a story more honest, or does it risk creating false equivalence? What obligation does a writer have when depicting real events?
Tap for discussion prompt
4
The title itself is a paradox: "everyday" and "movement" seem to contradict each other. By the end of the novel, how do you understand the title differently than when you started?
Think about how the protests transformed ordinary spaces—shopping malls, train stations, family dinner tables. When does the "everyday" become political? Is there a point in the novel where you can no longer separate the two?
Tap for discussion prompt
5
This book was banned from the Hong Kong Book Fair after political complaints. Does knowing that change how you read it? Should it?
Discuss the relationship between censorship and literature. Does a ban validate a book's importance? Does it change what you look for in the text? And what does it mean that this novel now reaches an English-speaking audience precisely because it was suppressed at home?
Tap for discussion prompt
6
The mother-daughter relationships are at the heart of this novel. How does Leung use the generational divide to mirror Hong Kong's own identity crisis?
The mothers lived through the handover; the daughters are fighting for a future that may already be foreclosed. Where do you see the generational tension in your own political context? Is the gap between "protect yourself" and "fight for your future" bridgeable?
Tap for discussion prompt
7
Leung juxtaposes the smell of tear gas with the smell of a mother's shampoo, violence with grocery shopping. What does this technique do to you as a reader?
Discuss how the kaleidoscopic structure—rapidly shifting between the intimate and the brutal—mirrors the actual experience of living through the protests. Did you find it disorienting? Powerful? Both? How does form serve content here?
Tap for discussion prompt
8
One reviewer called this novel "a love letter to Hong Kong's younger generation." Another called it a warning. Which reading do you lean toward—and can it be both?
Think about the emotional register of the ending. Does Leung leave you with hope, grief, admiration, or dread? Does the book celebrate the generation that protested, mourn what they lost, or simply bear witness?
Tap for discussion prompt
9
Consumerism runs through the novel like a second current: shopping malls, brand names, eating out. Is Leung critiquing consumer culture, or showing how it becomes a survival mechanism?
The novel opens at New Town Plaza, a real shopping mall that became a protest site. Consider how the "yellow economy" (pro-protest businesses) turned consumption itself into activism. What does it mean when where you buy your lunch is a political act?
Tap for discussion prompt
10
You're reading this novel in English, translated from Chinese, about a place most readers have never lived. What gets lost—and what gets found—in that distance?
Discuss the role of translation in political literature. Does reading this novel as an outsider give you clarity or create blind spots? What responsibility do we have as readers engaging with someone else's political crisis? And what did this book make you see differently about your own "everyday"?
Tap for discussion prompt
